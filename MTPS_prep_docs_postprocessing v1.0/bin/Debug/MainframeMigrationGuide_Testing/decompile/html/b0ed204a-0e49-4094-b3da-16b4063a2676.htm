Introduction to Modernization Analysis

Cognizant Technology Solutions offers Modernization Analysis to enable enterprises to assess their mainframe environments and to determine how they should respond to the major issues that confront them—for example, how to improve the flexibility, agility, IT process efficiency, and cost-effectiveness of their IT environments in an increasingly volatile business environment.

Modernization Analysis forms the first phase of a complete four-phase process for legacy modernization called Enterprise Architecture Integration Framework (EAIF) (see Figure 4.1). This first phase—Modernization Analysis—constitutes the A&D portion of EAIF. The following list provides an overview of the four phases of the framework:

  • Modernization Analysis. This phase consists of two parts: Business Domain Analysis and Portfolio Analysis. Business Domain Analysis analyzes the business processes of the enterprise and builds a high-level domain model of these business processes. Portfolio Analysis analyzes the legacy applications and identifies the optimal modernization strategy.

    Note:

    This guide is concerned only with Modernization Analysis. The remaining three phases of EAIF lie outside the scope of the guide. For the purposes of this guide, the expressions “Modernization Analysis” and “A&D Process” are interchangeable.

  • Modernization Definition. This phase consists of defining the future environment in terms of business processes, functionality, and architecture. The extraction of the business rules from legacy applications forms an important part of this phase.

  • Modernization Execution. This phase consists of implementing the modernization strategy adopted in the Modernization Analysis phase. Cognizant’s execution philosophy is to migrate the applications iteratively and incrementally to ensure that problems are detected and resolved early during this phase.

  • Modernization Go-Live. In this last phase of the modernization process, the migrated applications are tested and deployed as production systems. A wide range of tests—for example, functionality, system, migration, co-existence (of legacy and new systems), deployment, performance, stress and scalability, and usability—are carried out to ensure that the applications are production ready.

Business Domain Analysis

Business Domain Analysis forms the first part of Modernization Analysis. It focuses on the business processes supported by the legacy applications. It analyzes these business processes and constructs a high-level domain model of these processes. This domain model serves the following functions throughout the EAIF-based legacy modernization process:

  • Common platform. It presents a shared vision of the project in a language that is common to both the business stakeholders and technical professionals.

  • Business process improvements. It helps identify improvements in business processes that could be included in the modernization roadmap.

  • Stability of the domain model. The domain model presents a technology-independent picture of the enterprise’s business processes that remain the same regardless of the technology platform that supports them. This stability of the domain model across technology platforms serves as a guide for managing the changes that will be made to the existing environment. The domain model increases the efficiency of the modernization process by supporting a feature-driven approach to development.

  • SOA-based development. The domain model can be useful for identifying services that can be re-used to develop SOA-based applications.

Portfolio Analysis

Portfolio Analysis (PA) forms the second part of Modernization Analysis. It is a powerful and scientifically driven approach to help an organization analyze its IT applications portfolio and technology infrastructure and to suggest means and ways to optimize these applications through appropriate technology changes, including:

  • Identifying and recommending cost-effective technology solutions.

  • Providing roadmaps to realize short- and long-term business and technology objectives and goals.

  • Defining, designing, and implementing technology architecture to accommodate future business and technology needs and changes.

  • Arriving at an optimal, risk-free modernization plan to migrate the legacy applications to the chosen technology platform and applications environment.

Methodology of Portfolio Analysis

Cognizant combines a bottom-up approach focusing on technology architecture and process with a top-down investment plan to create an optimal implementation roadmap. It adopts a unique methodology for conducting portfolio analysis. This methodology consists of scientifically rigorous advanced statistical concepts and models, powerful empirical algorithms, and mathematical and other quantitative techniques to represent and analyze a given problem in an objective manner. Consequently, Cognizant’s recommendations are based on and supported by an analysis of the existing systems and their proposed future environment and its architecture.

The methodology of Portfolio Analysis generates three measures or metrics:

  • Ease-of-Porting Index. Data about the existing environment is grouped into categories and subjected to quantitative and qualitative analysis. The result of this analysis is the Ease-of-Porting Index that measures the difficulty of porting the applications to the new environment.

  • Dependency Index. Dependency analysis is carried out concurrently with Ease-of-Porting analysis. It analyzes the various functional dependencies within the existing applications and generates a Dependency Index.

  • Balanced Porting Scorecard. The two indexes are combined to generate the Balanced Porting Scorecard whereby each functionality in the existing application is assessed against a scale that measures the risk of migrating it to the target environment. This balanced scorecard, along with functional dependencies, is the foundation upon which Cognizant builds an optimal modernization implementation roadmap.

The Stages of Portfolio Analysis

The activities of Portfolio Analysis fall into six main stages:

  • Scope

  • Discovery

  • Portability Analysis

  • Migration Options

  • Impact Analysis

  • Recommendation and Presentation

Stage 1: Scope

The first stage consists of activities aimed at defining and finalizing the scope of the portfolio analysis project, including:

  • Project kickoff meeting.

  • Identifying the stakeholders.

  • Identifying systems and processes for analysis.

  • Establishing project boundaries and success criteria.

  • Creating the project agenda.

  • Developing a questionnaire to gather information about the systems.

  • Collecting and cataloguing all relevant system components and collating all source code.

  • Preparing the detailed plan for the project.

  • Documenting the complete scope and plan of the portfolio analysis project.

Stage 2: Discovery

The Discovery phase consists of collecting and analyzing the application data through different means such as a questionnaire, interviews, and source code analysis. The main activities in the Discovery phase are:

  • Analyzing the client’s documents dealing with the existing systems.

  • Identifying all applications and the business processes these applications implement.

  • Studying and reviewing the existing as-is architecture and data flows.

  • Understanding the detailed vision and the requirements of the future environment.

  • Identifying and understanding how the application code is distributed throughout the existing environment.

  • Interviewing the subject matter experts (SMEs).

At the end of this phase, the Cognizant team will possess a complete inventory of all the applications and data that are likely to have an impact on porting the applications to a new platform.

Stage 3: Portability Analysis

Portability Analysis focuses on how well an application will migrate to a new environment. It uses advanced statistical normalization techniques and algorithms to identify the most risk-prone applications in a scientific and objective manner. An important feature of Portability Analysis is that it does not merely consider applications individually but also takes into account the wide-ranging dependencies among these applications. Portability Analysis helps identify applications that:

  • Are mature and have a higher index on ease of porting. (Mature applications are applications that are stable and have been running in the system without recurring critical production issues.)

  • Should be retained without many changes.

  • Are under-performers and/or are overloaded and should therefore be re-engineered. (Under-performers are applications that are not giving the expected results in the legacy system, and there is no clear value or ROI in maintaining these applications in the legacy system. Hence they are good candidates for reengineering.)

Portability Analysis takes into account the project’s goals and constraints and combines them with the above metrics to arrive at a recommendation to adopt one of the following strategies for the applications:

  • Migrate

  • Re-architect and/or re-engineer

  • Scrap and re-code

  • New development

The Three Phases of Portability Analysis

Portability Analysis consists of three distinct phases:

  • Phase I: Ease-of-Porting (EOP) Index Calculation. This index measures how easily the application will migrate to the new environment.

  • Phase II: Dependency Index Calculation. This index measures how dependent an application is on other applications.

  • Phase III: Portability Score Determination. This metric is calculated by normalizing the values of the EOP Index and the Dependency Index. A high score indicates that the application is less risky and is therefore the most ideal candidate for porting; a low score indicates that careful planning will be required to prepare the application for porting.

The Portability Scores become the foundation for the migration roadmap and for computing the level of effort and cost of implementing the chosen migration strategy.

Stage 4: Migration Option Analysis

The objective of this analysis is to determine whether to maintain the applications on their current mainframe platform or to migrate them to another platform. The analysis seeks to answer the following questions about migration:

  • Will it lower hardware and software spending?

  • Will it reduce the cost of expanding the technology capacity of the new environment?

  • Will it overcome the key limitations of the existing applications?

  • Can the migration strategy use the existing resources and existing technologies on the new platform?

  • Does the implementation plan conform to the enterprise’s current strategic initiatives?

Choosing from the Migration Options

Cognizant evaluates the migration options by weighing the implementation costs, technology alignment, and functional value associated with each application. The options are:

  • Leave “as-is.” Migration will not add to the functionality of the application, nor will the application integrate seamlessly with the new environment in spite of new investment in it. On the other hand, the application is relatively easy to maintain.

  • Re-engineer. The application can be migrated to the new platform with moderate costs. Its functionality and integration with the new platform can be enhanced with moderate effort and costs.

  • Re-develop. Discard the existing code and re-develop the application from scratch on the new platform. However, the trade-off is that there is a higher cost/effort and risk involved with this option.

Stage 5: Impact Analysis

Impact Analysis focuses on identifying and determining the impact the migrated applications will have on the overall IT environment. To ensure that the impact analysis produces reliable results, both client and implementation teams are given overall control for the planning and management of porting the applications to the new environment. Impact analysis leads to more accurate effort and cost estimates and identifies peripheral applications that could cause problems during porting. Stage 6: Recommendations and Presentation

In this final stage of Portfolio Analysis, the project team presents the finalized portability model to the stakeholders and resolves issues through reviews and workshops.

The activities during this stage include:

  • Developing the order—tiers and grouping of applications—in which applications will be ported (see Figure 4.5 for a sample grouping of applications).

  • Proposing the migration implementation roadmap.

  • Developing the methodology and plan for porting the applications.

  • Developing the effort and cost estimates.

  • Conducting workshops and reviews with the customer team to finalize the recommendations.

  • Presenting the finalized recommendations and roadmap to the stakeholders.

  • Closing the project.
